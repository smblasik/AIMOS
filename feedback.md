Draft clear, objective, and actionable **written** feedback using the SBI framework. Use this skill when asked to write feedback for a colleague or direct report — whether in an email to their manager or as an entry in a performance management system (Workday, etc.).

## The SBI Framework

Feedback content is always structured in three sequential parts:

### S — Situation
Describe the specific context. Be precise about time, place, and circumstances so it's clear exactly when and where this happened.

### B — Behavior
Describe only what was observable — what a video recording would capture. Stick to facts: what was said or done. Avoid:
- Judgments ("they were careless")
- Assumptions about intent ("they didn't care")
- Labels or character assessments ("they're disorganized")

### I — Impact
Explain the effect of the behavior across three dimensions:
- **On you** — what you thought or felt
- **On others** — how others reacted
- **On results** — impact on the team, project, or business

## Output Formats

### Format A: Email to Someone Else's Manager

Use when you're sharing feedback about a colleague with their manager. The manager will likely discuss it with the person, so the SBI should still be direct and factual, but written from your perspective as a peer/observer.

**Structure:**

```
Subject: Feedback on [Name] — [positive/area for growth]

Hi [Manager],

[Context sentence — why you're writing.]

[Situation] [Behavior] [Impact — concise, 1-2 sentences total.]

Happy to discuss further if helpful.

Best,
[Your Name]
```

**Reinforcing example:**
> Hi Sarah,
>
> I wanted to share some positive feedback on Jen.
>
> During yesterday's Q3 planning session when the team was debating resource allocation, Jen paused the conversation and proposed a clear framework for ranking priorities based on client impact. She then facilitated the group through each item without overriding anyone's input.
>
> I thought it showed real leadership — the team landed on a decision in 15 minutes instead of the usual back-and-forth. It moved our planning forward by a full day.
>
> Happy to discuss further if helpful.

**Constructive example:**
> Hi Sarah,
>
> I wanted to share an observation that might help with Jen's development.
>
> During Tuesday's client presentation around 3pm when Mark asked about the revised timeline, Jen paused for about 10 seconds and then said she'd need to check and get back to him.
>
> I felt it undercut confidence in our preparation — Mark seemed surprised we didn't have that answer ready, and it pushed the decision timeline out by a week. I think with a bit more prep on likely curveballs, Jen could handle those moments more smoothly.
>
> Happy to discuss.

### Format B: Performance Management System Entry (Workday, etc.)

Use when entering feedback into a formal review tool. These entries are read by the reviewee and HR, and are often used to justify ratings. They should be self-contained, professional, and impact-weighted.

**Structure:**
- Write in paragraph form unless the system has specific fields
- Lead with the behavior and impact (situation can be implied if already clear from context)
- Use bullet points if the system supports them for multiple examples
- Avoid conversational openers — get straight to the substance
- Third-person where appropriate

**Reinforcing entry:**
> [Name] consistently drives team alignment in high-pressure situations. In Q3 planning when the team was debating resource allocation, they paused the discussion and introduced a priority-ranking framework based on client impact. They facilitated the group through each item without overriding dissenting views. As a result, the team reached consensus in 15 minutes and moved planning ahead by a full day. This kind of structured facilitation elevates the whole team's output.

**Constructive entry:**
> [Name] would benefit from deepening preparation for executive presentations. In the Q3 client review when asked about the revised timeline, they were unable to answer and deferred. The client appeared to lose confidence in our readiness, and the decision was pushed out by a week. Focusing on anticipating likely questions before senior-level meetings would help project greater ownership and credibility.

## Workflow

1. **Clarify the format** — Ask: is this an email to a manager, or a PM system entry? Who is the subject?
2. **Gather the raw content** — Elicit the situation, the specific behavior, and the impact using SBI prompts
3. **Choose the right level of formality** — Email to manager: conversational but professional. PM system: more formal, standalone, third-person where appropriate.
4. **Draft and present** — Show the draft for approval before sending/pasting anywhere
5. **Iterate** — Adjust tone, length, or emphasis based on feedback

## Rules & Constraints

1. **No mind-reading** — Never assume or state what someone was thinking or intended. Stick to observable behavior.
2. **No "you always/you never"** — Stay specific to concrete events, not patterns.
3. **Timing matters** — Reference recent, specific events with enough detail to be credible.
4. **Own your perspective** — In email to a manager, "I felt..." or "I noticed..." is appropriate. In PM system entries, lead with the behavior and impact directly.
5. **No surprises** — Written feedback going to a manager or into a formal system should generally reflect feedback the person has already heard informally, unless specified otherwise.
6. **Keep it scannable** — Short paragraphs, one idea each. Managers and reviewers read quickly.
7. **Balance counts** — Reinforcing and constructive are equally important. The SBI structure works for both.
